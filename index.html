Epsilon · 2021 → present · Staff Software Engineer · Tech lead

AI Creative Production Platform

A production creative tool for people making real ads, shaped from messy workflow ambiguity into AI-assisted concepting, layout, editing, and brand-quality evals.

The ambiguous ask

Creative production had outgrown the tools around it. Every new ad variation still asked a designer to do careful, repetitive production work inside Illustrator and Photoshop. The opportunity was not just to add AI; it was to define a product workflow where brand fidelity, usability, and creative control could survive at much higher volume.

The product shape

I led the product architecture for a web-based builder that turns creative intent into production-ready variations. The work sat between product, design, and engineering: preserve what designers meant, make the UX feel direct, and give AI enough structure to be helpful. A homegrown agentic orchestration loop over tiered Gemini models handles concepting, layout, and edits, while production-data bridges — like real-time font-ID lookups — keep output tied to the actual brand system.

The production layer

I built a reusable eval skill so AI features could be judged against the same things creative teams care about: layout accuracy, color fidelity, and visual drift against baselines. A meta-optimizer reads those scores and tunes the prompts, model tier, pipeline steps, and schemas. It turns taste into something the system can keep checking, not something everyone has to rediscover by eye.

The launch signal

The system is in production, fully adopted across the creative org, and now moving into an agent-first refactor. The eval harness stays the trust layer between speed and quality as deterministic orchestration becomes more flexible agentic workflow.

Epsilon · 2017 → present · Founder & UX engineering lead

Core UI — shared infrastructure for product quality

A production design system that translated design intent into accessible, reusable product infrastructure across Figma, Storybook, and shipped code.

The design gap

Designers and engineers were doing good work in places that did not stay connected. Designs lived in Figma, components were rebuilt per product, accessibility arrived too late, and every team solved the same interface problems again. The gap between design intent and production reality was where quality quietly leaked out.

The engineering bridge

I founded the UX Engineering discipline at Epsilon and grew what started as a side project into the company's shared design system. The job was part systems architecture, part design translation, and part patient relationship-building: sit with designers, understand the intent, then make the component real enough that engineers could ship it consistently. Tokens, patterns, and documentation lived alongside the code, where they could keep earning trust.

The production layer

I treated WCAG 2.1 AA as a design constraint, embedded into every component — not a checklist run at audit. Color contrast, focus order, ARIA semantics, keyboard paths: all part of the material. The same pattern shows up in my AI work now: invest in the substrate once, and every team downstream gets a better starting point.

The launch signal

Core UI is still the foundation under a lot of the work I care about. It is where taste, accessibility, and system thinking became shared infrastructure; the AI tooling is where that same way of thinking now gets pointed at agents. Live at coreui.epsilon.com (opens in a new tab).

MakeMeA — a fast product bet with useful lessons

An AI photo-style app built in 48 hours, useful as proof of how quickly I can move from product idea to UX, implementation, live launch, and autonomous growth loop.

The product wedge

I pair-programmed with agents from empty repo to live URL in 48 hours. Next.js, TypeScript, OpenAI, and a deliberately small product surface. Every design decision was about shipability: keep the flow obvious, tune each style with its own prompt, make the watermark a single overlay step, and avoid infrastructure that would slow the idea down.

The system around it

The longer-term bet was the engine around the app. MakeMeA runs a trend-to-publish pipeline without human intervention: find an emerging visual style, spin up the prompt and parameters, generate samples, produce short-form videos, and write SEO/AEO posts. The product became a wrapper around a system that can keep making its own discovery surfaces.

The launch signal

Live, functional, not profitable. The market is crowded, and competitors give away plenty of what I charge for. Still, it validated AI-native velocity as a repeatable baseline, and the auto-content infrastructure is more interesting than the product it happens to wrap. See it live →

What I learned

The lesson was useful and humbling: shipping fast is a capability, not a guarantee. Taste only stays differentiated if the system can keep up with culture, and product-market fit still has to be earned the slow human way.

How I Work

How I turn ambiguity into launched systems

  • Clarify the product shape

    I start by turning fuzzy ideas into constraints, workflows, risks, and the smallest useful thing worth building.

  • Make the UX tangible

    Flows, states, prototypes, and component behavior make the idea concrete enough for design, product, and engineering to react to the same thing.

  • Build with the system in mind

    The first version should teach the architecture: where AI belongs, where deterministic code belongs, and what needs to become reusable infrastructure.

  • Measure what good means

    I like beautiful guesses, but I trust measured improvement. The best AI teams know what good looks like and keep tuning toward it.

  • Earn trust before autonomy

    I ship the human-in-the-loop version first, learn where judgment matters, then give agents more room as the safety rails prove themselves.

  • Harden the last mile

    Production quality is where the work becomes real: accessible UI, edge cases, data plumbing, evals, handoff, and systems that keep running.

  • Package the useful parts

    When something works, I turn it into a reusable skill, component, or pattern so the next team starts further ahead.

  • Use agents as part of the craft

    I use Claude Code, Cursor, MCP, committed agent docs, and published skills as part of the way I build. The interesting shift is the tighter loop between designer, engineer, and agent.
